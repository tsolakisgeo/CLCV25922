History of Mt. Lykaion

Situated in the middle of the Peloponnese, Mt. Lykaion was an important sanctuary and hotbed of activity for ancient Arcadians. The mountain receives its name from the mythological King Lycaon, who was turned to a wolf by Zeus as punishment for feeding him human flesh—Book I of Ovid's Metamorphoses discusses the "lycanthropy" at great length. The earliest human activity is thought to be during the Mycenean age, as there is evidence for an ancient sanctuary at the mountain's ash altar. Nevertheless, Lykaion achieved its greatest prominence during the height of the Peloponnesian War; the Neda River flowed nearby, and Lykaion sat near the borders of Arcadia, Messinia, and Elis. The cult of Zeus Lykaios flourished in the Classical Era, and the famed Lykaian games were held at the Hippodrome—though not as important as its competition at Olympia, the winners of the Lykaian Games could take great pride in their achievements. The site is divided into two key areas. The Upper Sanctuary, at the top of the mountain, is the site of the ash altar and the ancient Temenos, where it was said that anyone bold enough to enter would die within the year. Meanwhile, the Lower Sanctuary —about 200 meters down-held the ancient Hippodrome, the Agno Fountain, and the Sanctuary of Pan. Even from the Lower Sanctuary, the view is remarkable, but on a clear day one can see all of the Peloponnesian peaks from the altar. Much of what we know about ancient Lykaion comes from the work of Pausanias, an ancient traveller living during the 2nd century CE. The mountain had lost its importance by the Roman ages, but Pausanias still writes with great reverence for the ancient customs that once defined the site. Pausanias is one of a few extant sources concerning Lykaian ritual, and his account proved vital for inspiring an excavation at the site. The first major excavations of Lykaion were led by Kourouniotis at the turn of the 20th century. He placed trenches by the temenos and throughout the Lower Sanctuary, finding a swath of cultural material from the Classical Ages and beyond. The current excavation, led by Dr. Romano and Dr. Voyatzis, was started in 2004 to expand on the earlier findings of Kourouniotis, and in particular to learn more about the stadium and the ancient Lykaian games. The first report from the excavation is here on JSTOR.

Trench PP

The excavation opened Trench PP in 2016 to explore a "circular anomaly" that had been seen on Google Earth. The Sanctuary of Pan, as Pausanias had described it, was supposed to be within the Lower Sanctuary, but no location had been determined until the anomaly was discovered. In 2019, a painted inscription to Pan was found in the middle of the trench, giving backing to Pausanias' observations; the red-figure pottery found within the trench was also a valuable find. Our goal heading into the 2022 season was to look for more evidence in support of the Sanctuary, and to look for some kind of water structure that would have supported a grove of trees. The following images are all maps constructed in QGIS, using different kinds of layers and CSV files to illustrate what we found. Before I could start building my map, I needed to go through my data. Archaeologists keep both digital and physical records of everything they find, but I was working off of digital PDFs of our scanned notebooks. I went through both notebooks, writing down elevation points for our significant finds and screeshotting the added photos of them, and additionally keeping track of what we found in each basket. Once I had all my data, I created CSV files for my four layers, including a pathname column that linked the coordinate to the photo. I then began building my QGIS file. I started off by adding the latest Google Satellite data as a basemap. I created a new XYZ Layer, sourced the Satellite data from online, maximized my zoom level (as we were working within a small area), and established a new connection. I then added the drone photo of the trench on top of the satellite imagery. I had to pick a coordinate that was approximately at the middle of the drone photo, and make small changes if my placement was off (which I had to do for practically every CSV file, since the excavaton notebooks only contained elevations for our baskets/finds). I rotated the photo to match the North orientation, and lowered the opacity. I then repeated that process for the photos of significant finds and the basket maps. For all three CSVs, I added a Raster Image Marker that used a linked photo to represent the coordinate. I was able to directly align the pathname column in each CSV with the Raster. For the map of the density of cultural material finds, I used a marker that we had used in class with the Crete map: a Graduated marker. I created lots of classes based on the value in the Cultural Material column, and set a range of sizes for the markers depending on the Cultural Material value. The main problem here was having to re-adjust the coordinates constantly to match up the photos. Thankfully, QGIS gives you coordinates if you right-click.

Converting Coordinate Values to UTM Values

GPS coordinate values are helpful for locating something on the Earth, but they aren't helpful when you want to work with a flat map, since the distance covered by degrees of longitude and latitude isn't constant (because of the Earth's curvature). UTM Coordinates, known as Universal Transverse Mercator, keep a constant distance between coordinates, and allow points to be better represented on an XYZ axis. Conclusions

I wanted to create a project that shows how technology can aid the Classics, and I think I've been able to do that. QGIS makes it possible for archaeologists to visualize their findings and create different layers that illuminate what they've discovered; it's also possible for satellite imagery to inspire new discoveries, as it did for my trench. DataFrames, meanwhile, make it possible to deal with an overwhelming amount of numbers and to sort or convert them if another academic party should need their data for something else. My biggest takeaway from this project, however, is that Classicists need to place greater importance on technology in general. Archaeologists prioritize written notebooks above all else, which is easy to access if you have the physical copy, but it becomes a pain once you're dealing with fifty baskets and hundreds of elevation points, and you can't write a code to search them up.
